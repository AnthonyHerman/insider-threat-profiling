# Model comparison: transparent detector vs. a learned baseline

This document records a head-to-head between the platform's production detector — a
**transparent additive model** ([`model.rs`](../plugins/plugin-agent-detect/src/model.rs))
— and a **learned logistic-regression baseline**
([`baseline.rs`](../plugins/plugin-agent-detect/src/baseline.rs)). The goal is to
answer a fair, sceptical question: *does a model that is free to fit the data do
materially better than hand-calibrated coefficients and physiological hard rules?*

## TL;DR

On synthetic data the two models are **statistically indistinguishable** at low and
moderate evasion, the learned baseline holds a **tiny (~0.02 AUC) edge** at high
evasion (`e = 0.75`), and **both collapse to chance** under a perfect behavioural
mimic (`e = 1.0`). Because the learned baseline can overfit the synthetic
generator, that small mid-budget edge is **not** evidence of field skill. We
therefore keep the transparent model in production: at equivalent accuracy,
explainability is decisive for an insider-threat tool.

## How the comparison is run (and why it is fair)

Reproduce the table with:

```text
cargo run --release -p plugin-agent-detect --example model_comparison
```

The harness ([`eval::compare_models_across_evasion`](../plugins/plugin-agent-detect/src/eval.rs))
is engineered so the comparison is honest and leak-free:

1. **Same feature pipeline.** Every session — for both training and testing, for
   both models — is generated by [`synth`](../plugins/plugin-agent-detect/src/synth.rs)
   and passed through the **real** `SessionAccumulator::features()` extraction. No
   feature value is hand-rolled. Under-evidenced sessions (`features() == None`) are
   dropped identically for both models, so they see the same evidenced population.
2. **Train once, on naive data.** The logistic regression is fit on naive
   (`e = 0`) `human()` vs `agent()` sessions only. The evader is *adaptive and
   unknown at training time*, mirroring the production threat model; training per
   evasion budget would let the LR trivially memorise each one.
3. **Shared, disjoint held-out test set.** For each evasion budget `e`, one test set
   is drawn from `human()` vs the evaded agent (`evade(e)`) with a **distinct RNG
   seed** so train/test draws never overlap.
4. **Identical scoring.** Both models score the **same** held-out rows, and both AUCs
   come from the **same** estimator, [`eval::roc_auc`](../plugins/plugin-agent-detect/src/eval.rs)
   (Mann–Whitney U, ties averaged). Same rows, same labels, same AUC ⇒ apples-to-apples.

### The learned baseline in one paragraph

An ordinary logistic regression in `f64`, trained by deterministic full-batch
gradient descent (zero-init weights, fixed data order, no RNG in the optimizer) on
binary cross-entropy with an L2 penalty on the weights (bias unregularized). Inputs
are standardized with statistics fit on the training set only. The one subtlety is
**missing data**: the four Tier-3 features (`gap_autocorr`, `think_tail_ratio`,
`throughput_decay`, `keystroke_burst_cv`) are legitimately `NaN` below the robust
command gate (see [`MIN_COMMANDS_ROBUST`](../plugins/plugin-agent-detect/src/features.rs)).
The transparent model handles this by dropping the term and renormalizing; a
fixed-weight LR cannot. We use the standard leak-free fix — **mean-imputation with a
missingness indicator**, both fit on train only — so a short session's absent Tier-3
evidence is represented honestly rather than read as a real measurement.

## Results

Synthetic, reproducible. Each cell averages seeds `[1, 2, 3, 4, 5]`; train = 600
agent + 600 human naive sessions; held-out test = 400 agent + 400 human sessions per
budget. The *evasion budget* `e` interpolates an automated agent's behaviour toward a
human's (`e = 0` naive agent, `e = 1` perfect behavioural mimic). Agent = positive
class.

| evasion e | transparent AUC | learned-baseline AUC | Δ (transparent − learned) |
|----------:|----------------:|---------------------:|--------------------------:|
| 0.00 | 1.0000 | 1.0000 | +0.0000 |
| 0.25 | 1.0000 | 1.0000 | +0.0000 |
| 0.50 | 0.9995 | 1.0000 | −0.0005 |
| 0.75 | 0.9446 | 0.9640 | −0.0193 |
| 1.00 | 0.5095 | 0.5110 | −0.0015 |

(At `e = 1.0` both AUCs sit on the ~0.5 chance line; single-seed values scatter to
either side of 0.5 — e.g. 0.47–0.53 — which is exactly the sampling noise expected
when two indistinguishable distributions are ranked.)

## Discussion

**The two models are competitive across the whole evasion range.** Through `e = 0.5`
they are effectively tied at the ceiling. The only visible gap is at `e = 0.75`,
where the learned baseline leads by ~0.02 AUC, and at `e = 1.0` both are at chance.
The transparent model — hand-calibrated, with no access to the labels — keeps pace
with a model fit directly to the data.

**The learned baseline's mid-budget edge is most likely generator overfit, not field
skill.** This is the central caveat and it must travel with every number above. The
logistic regression is trained on data from the *same generator* it is tested
against. Even with a strictly disjoint train/test split, it can learn the
generator's idiosyncrasies — the exact log-normal parameters, the variance-preserving
AR(1) think-time process, the fatigue drift — rather than behaviour that would
generalize to real operators and real agents. A ~0.02 AUC advantage at one budget is
precisely the size of effect one expects from such overfitting, and it would not
survive a distribution shift to field data. We deliberately do **not** claim, and the
unit tests deliberately do **not** assert, that the learned model "wins."

**Both models collapse under a perfect mimic — as theory requires.** At `e = 1.0` the
evader reproduces the human's marginals *and* temporal structure, so the two classes
are behaviourally identical and no scorer can separate them. This is not a weakness of
either model; it is the correct result, and it confirms that the evasion story is a
property of the *problem*, not an artifact of the transparent model's particular
coefficients. The value of the detector is the **cost** it imposes on an adversary to
reach that point (surrendering throughput, serial correlation, and error-free speed).

**Why the transparent model stays in production.** For an insider-threat tool, a
verdict that cannot be explained is operationally useless: an analyst must be able to
see *why* a session was flagged, and a subject must be able to contest it. The
transparent model emits ranked, named reasons
([`Detection::reasons`](../crates/aegis-sdk)) — `reaction-time-floor`,
`whole-line-injection`, `uncorrelated-flat-throughput`, etc. — each tied to a
documented physiological or behavioural prior. The logistic regression emits a
single probability and a vector of standardized weights that an on-call analyst
cannot readily defend in an HR or legal proceeding. Given **equivalent** measured
accuracy on this benchmark, explainability is the deciding factor. The transparent
model also encodes priors (the ~150 ms reaction floor as a hard rule, the asymmetric
"can only raise agent evidence" ratchet) that bound its worst-case false-positive
behaviour by construction — guarantees a free-fitting LR does not provide.

## What this comparison is and is not

* **It is** a cross-check that the production model's hand-crafted priors are
  *competitive* with a model free to fit the data, scored fairly on a shared held-out
  set — and a demonstration that the evasion trade-off holds for both model classes.
* **It is not** a claim of field accuracy for *either* model. Both are evaluated on
  synthetic sessions sampled from documented behavioural distributions, not on
  telemetry collected from instrumented humans and agents in the field. A field study
  with IRB-approved data collection — and a re-derivation of the model's
  FPR-budgeted thresholds against the deployment's own human population — is future
  work, tracked alongside the synthetic-evaluation limitations in
  [`docs/paper`](paper).

## Reproducibility

* Comparison table: `cargo run --release -p plugin-agent-detect --example model_comparison`
* Headline properties are asserted in CI by
  `eval::tests::transparent_competitive_with_learned_baseline` (both AUCs > 0.9 at
  `e = 0`; the two models within a 0.10 AUC band at every budget; both degrade from
  naive to mimic and approach chance at `e = 1`).
* The learned baseline's own behaviour is covered by the `baseline::tests` unit tests
  (separation of naive agents at AUC > 0.9, standardized-column statistics, a finite
  probability on a short `NaN`-bearing vector, and bit-identical determinism across
  re-training).
